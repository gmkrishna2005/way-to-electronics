What is VLSI?

VLSI stands for Very Large Scale Integration. It refers to the process of creating integrated circuits by combining thousands to millions of transistors onto a single chip. VLSI technology is the foundation of modern digital devices such as microprocessors, memory chips, and application-specific ICs.

Key Points:

  • Enables complex functionality in small form factors
  • Reduces power consumption and cost per function
  • Used in CPUs, GPUs, SoCs, and more

Back to Learn

CMOS Technology

CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) is a technology for constructing integrated circuits. It uses both p-type and n-type MOSFETs to achieve low static power consumption and high noise immunity.

Applications:

  • Microprocessors
  • Microcontrollers
  • Static RAM

Digital Logic Design

Digital logic design is the basis of digital circuits. It involves the use of logic gates (AND, OR, NOT, etc.) to implement combinational and sequential circuits.
Topics include:

  • Boolean algebra
  • Combinational logic
  • Sequential logic
  • State machines

ASIC vs FPGA

ASIC (Application-Specific Integrated Circuit) is a custom chip designed for a specific application, while FPGA (Field-Programmable Gate Array) is a reconfigurable chip that can be programmed after manufacturing.
Comparison:

  • ASIC: High performance, low unit cost (in volume), not reprogrammable
  • FPGA: Flexible, reprogrammable, higher unit cost, good for prototyping
